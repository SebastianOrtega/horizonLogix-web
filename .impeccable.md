## Design Context

### Users

Two buyer personas, weighted equally — design must satisfy both at once:

- **The operator (ops/plant/warehouse director).** Has a known pain (RFID reads not landing in their ERP, contenedores untraceable, WIP blind spots). Lands here to confirm Horizon Logix solves their *specific* operational problem. Skims for vertical fit ("does this work for food & bev?"), reader compatibility, ERP integration. Decision driven by trust, not enthusiasm.
- **The IT lead / integrator.** Evaluating middleware on behalf of a client or for an internal stack. Skims for protocols, plugin architecture, deployment shape (on-prem? cloud? edge?), API surface. Decision driven by technical credibility, not marketing copy.

Both arrive expecting *engineering-grade seriousness*. The site loses them the moment it reads as a SaaS landing aimed at venture-backed software buyers. They want a vendor who has clearly handled real industrial floors.

Mexico-anchored (Spanish default, LFPDPPP-aware) but the EN side must hold up to a US/EU integrator audit.

### Brand Personality

**Industrial. Literary. A little defiant.**

- *Industrial* — built, not branded. References technical manuals, trade publications, engineering catalogs, and Apple's industrial product pages (precision typography at scale, generous whitespace with intent, photography of materials and machinery, dense specs presented beautifully).
- *Literary* — sentences that take a position. Editorial pull-quotes. Serif at scale. The site has a voice; it doesn't recite feature bullets.
- *A little defiant* — refuses the SaaS-landing template. Asymmetric. Long-scroll. Alternating dark/light section rhythm instead of one cohesive theme. Confident enough to leave space empty.

Emotional goal: the visitor closes the tab thinking *these people know what they're doing* — not *this is a beautiful site*.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Reference**: Apple Industrial. Tight type tracking at huge scale, restrained color, precision in every detail, photography that respects materials. Not Vercel/Linear/Stripe SaaS-modernism, not generic startup minimalism.

**Anti-references**: glassmorphism, neon-on-dark with cyan accents, gradient-text headlines, identical-card grids, "AI dashboard" tropes, hero-with-glowing-orb compositions, every-button-primary, generic icon-above-heading templates.

**Theme**: alternating dark/light section rhythm preserved as a structural commitment, not a default. Dark sections (`ink-950`/`ink-900`) carry the editorial weight; light sections (`paper-50`) carry the legibility-first technical detail. The alternation is part of the brand — keep it.

**Typography** (planned swap from current Fraunces + Inter):
- Display: **Young Serif** — wide, slab-influenced, feels like an industrial nameplate. Headline-only.
- Body: **Schibsted Grotesk** — engineered Norwegian newspaper grotesk, restrained with subtle character. Replaces Inter.
- Mono: **JetBrains Mono** — keeps. Used for technical specs, tabular numerics, code-adjacent moments. Not as a "developer vibes" decoration — only where actual technical content lives.
- Pairing logic: literary serif at the edges (where the design *talks*), engineered sans at the center (where the design *works*).

**Color**:
- Surfaces: `ink-950` `#0B0F1A` and `paper-50` `#F7F7F5`. Tinted neutrals only — never pure black/white.
- Accent: `#f6891a` amber-orange (`src/theme.js` is the single source of truth). One accent, used sparingly. Earns its visibility by appearing rarely — primary CTAs, active states, the data-flow signal in animated diagrams. Never decorative.
- Note: `src/index.css` still has stale blue tokens from a prior pass — they should be reconciled with `theme.js` during the polish pass.

**Motion**: animations *explain* (data flow, ingest behavior, state changes). Decorative motion is rejected. Existing patterns (`pulse-ring`, `flow`, `scroll-up`, `drift`, the workflow designer ticker) earn their place because they illustrate how the middleware works. New motion must clear the same bar. All motion gated on `prefers-reduced-motion`.

**Layout**: cards and shadows are last-resort containers. Hierarchy comes from typographic scale, spacing rhythm, and section-tone alternation — not from nested boxes. Existing varied spacing patterns stay; resist any urge to flatten everything into a uniform grid.

### Design Principles

1. **Industrial editorial over SaaS slick.** Reference technical manuals and trade publications. Reject Vercel/Linear-style polished SaaS minimalism.
2. **Defiance through restraint.** Refuse template patterns (every-button-primary, identical card grids, hero-with-orb). Confidence shows in what's left out.
3. **Literary at the edges, engineered at the center.** Serif where the design talks (headlines, pull-quotes, captions). Sans where it works (UI, dense lists, specs).
4. **One accent, used sparingly.** Amber `#f6891a` only signals action or state. Rarity is what makes it readable.
5. **Motion explains, never decorates.** If an animation doesn't illustrate a real system behavior, it doesn't ship.
6. **Tabular numerics everywhere data appears.** Specs, latencies, throughputs, dates — monospace-aligned. Industrial precision shows in columns of numbers being readable as columns.
7. **Bilingual lockstep.** ES and EN must hit the same beats. No copy lives in one language and not the other.
8. **Trust the alternating tone rhythm.** Dark/light section alternation is structural, not decorative. New sections find their place in the rhythm.
